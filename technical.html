TECHNICAL BACKGROUND

DATA

Seeker's prcing data is provided by Commodity Systems, Inc. with we have had an excellent relationship for more than 10 years. Our data set includes active Wilshire5000 stocks (currently numbering 3600+), 1600+ Equity Indexes, 1300 ETFs .  Analysis is based on daily closing prices.

Comparison of vendor data base values between two entities can be complicated where one or other has limited data in a given period. To address this, Target and Match data are locked through a proprietary mathod that validates data dates against true trading dates. Values are entered against these trading dates and are only processed if they are matching pairs. Comparisons are generally discarded if data are not available for 75% of the period measured. 

Averages are based on valid non-zero values. Medians include all values. 

Important results should always be checked before use. When checking results, note that a gap in data may slide later values so that they no longer match, invalidating the analysis. Should you find an important result that is of concern, please provide the data and we will be pleased to confirm the analysis for you. 

PORTFOLIO STYLE ANALYSIS- CORRELATION

The selected portfolio is correlated by interval against each index in the system. The indexes are sorted into categories (an index may be in more than one category, e.g. DJ US RealEstate will be included in the index group DJ and the group RealEstate). The average correlation for each category is averaged by interval, and the average correlation of each group is charted over time.

STOCK AND INDEX GROUPS

The IIL standard Stock Groups are based on industry codes. IIL standard Index groups are based on the presence of the group name in the full name of the index.

 Ticker symbols are occasionally reused; the same symbol can, for example, be used for two (or more) different companies at different times and in different sectors ( e.g. HOG). When you use ticker or index groups, they are identified by tickerid and are unambiguous, but when you enter a stock ticker, Seeker processes all entities that match that symbol at any point in the time period that you have chosen. This may lead to output in which the symbols are listed but no correlations occur in the period you have selected.

To find out which ticker symbols are contained in a stock or index group, select the group as a Target, and run Autocorrelation, which lists both the stock tickers and their full names.

INTERVALS

Analysis is divided into subintervals to show how overall correlation might vary over shorter periods. This can appear to be volatile because the correlation coefficient includes not only the product of the two variables being compared; it is divided by their standard deviations which become more volatile when the sample size is small, and we recommend not using intervals of less than a month (20 trading days). 

In order to help understand sub-itnterval variability we present a group of measurements. They include the average, high and low, the median, and the standard deviation of the correlation value over the subintervals. You may notice that some pairs correlate very well over the entire period, but show a wide difference in standard deviation over subintervals. When the overall correlation is high and the standard deviation is small, confidence in the overall correlation can be higher. With the exception of the first and last subintervals, each extends from the first trading day of the month to the last trading day of the month. The first subinterval starts on the first trading day on or following the From Date and includes that month. The last subinterval ends on the To Date.

Phase offsets are measured in days ahead. For example, if stock is correlated with an index with a phase difference of 20, the most recent stock is paired with the index data from 20 days ago, etc. If correlation improves over a phase offset, the index is seen as leading (or predicting) the stock.

MATH

Seeker reports the Pearson product-moment correlation coefficient (ρ is usually used to refer to the measurement over a complete population, while r is used to represent measurements over a subset of the population. We refer to both as ρ in this discussion). ρ varies between +1, indicating perfect correlation; 0, reflecting no correlaton; and -1 indicating that the two distributions move orthogonally. It is calculated as follows (for reference see Pearson product-moment) and related Wikipedia articles that discuss interpretation.

    ρ = (Exy-(Ex*Ey))/(σx * σy) where Ex = expected value of x = (1/N)∑xi etc; xi, yi are matched pairs of data points at the same date, and N is the count of each.

σ is the standard deviation of xi or yi calculated by using Welford's formula for the variance calculated as

    σ^2 = (1/(N*(N-1)))*[ n*Sum (xi^2) - (Sum xi)^2] 

Covariance and correlation coefficients are calculated using N.

The regression used for factor analysis calculates a linear relationship between the factors, and the expected return over the risk-free-rate, R where 

    R = F0 + F1*RmMRfr + F2*SMB + F3*HML adding, in the 5 factor case F4*RMW + F5*CMA where F0 represents alpha, and F2 etc represent the coefficients of the factors.

It can be seen that F0 represents the expected return when all the factors are zero, and that F1, for example, represents the change expected from a 1 unit change in RmMPfr, etc. The signs of the coefficients is important, indicating whether R changes in the same direction as a given factor. 

Seeker outputs the R2, the fraction of the variation in the result that is accounted for by the independent factors. Generally more important are the coefficients, their Standard Errors (equivalent to the standard deviation on their values), and the Probability, which measures the likelihood that the coefficient could not be zero i.e. its significance. The statistical analysis measures the assumed relationship against the possibility that the coefficients could be zero. The Probabilities show the probability that the factor coefficients are valid, and not zero: 95% is usually regarded as a solid indicator. 

TESTING SIGNIFICANCE

In factor analysis, the significance of factor weighting can be measured in terms of the probability that it is large in relation to random fluctuations of a variable that is really zero. Significance testing is based on a "T" distribution where the T value is the ratio of the weighting to the standard error of each factor.  Seeker transforms this ratio into a probability, and 95% is usually taken to indicate that the weighting value is significant. 

In correlation the hypothesis that the correlation coefficient is significant  entails calculating

t = ρ/√[(1 - ρ^2)/(N - 2)] where N, the number of measurements, is greater than 6. ρ is the correlation coefficient.

t can be compared to the values of a "t" distribution in testing the hypothesis that the measurement comes from a larger population in which ρ = 0. i.e. one in which there is NOT a linear relationship. For example, using N = 20, which is the typical value for a Seeker month, then a value t > 2.1 indicates that there is only a 5% probability that the data are NOT linked by a linear relationship. A value of 2.9 is significant at the 1% level.

Translating these to values of ρ for monthly intervals that you can read from Seeker's grids, ρ >= 0.48 is significant at the 5%, and ρ >= 0.67 at the 1% level.


CALCULATING ALPHA AND BETA

Seeker calculates relative gain and relative volatility over intervals. Relative gain is the ratio of closing price at the end of the interval to closing price on day before the first day, divided by the same ratio for the Matching stock or index. Note that high ratios can occur when the Match has a very small change in price over the period.

Relative volatility is the standard deviation of the Target over the interval divided by that of the Match. Note that very high ratios can occur when comparing to a Match that has a standard deviation close to zero.
