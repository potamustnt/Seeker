HOW CAN SEEKER HELP ME?

Factor Analysis

Seeker uses two mathematical methods, correlation and regression, applying them to changes in value between pairs of investment products, and to analyzing factors (other than management skill) that might be contributing to changes in value. Correlation measures similarity of performance but can not explain why it happens: we can find which stocks tend to have similar performance, or which indexes behave like selected stocks, and we can show how these similarlties are preserved over time, but not why. Regression enables us to compare performance against parameters that might be contributory, and is used to try to isolate whether changes in performance are unique to the entity and its management, or may be explained by factors that are known to influence performance, such as the fact that small cap stocks tend to outperform large caps. This analysis depends on defining which factors might be useful, and on data that enables us to isolate their effects. 

Several factor analysis models have been proposed; the best known is the Fama-French 3-Factor model, created by Professors Eugene Fama and Kenneth French at the University of Chicago. The model was extended to 5 factors in 2015, and Seeker implements both. Their analysis draws on the results of their research that indicates that a major component of performance can be attributed to company size, market risk, and price-to-book ratios. Their analysis measures performance in relation to the market (return of the market minus risk-free rate, which we describe as RmMRfr); and the extent to which low price-to-book stocks (i.e. high book-to-market, a.k.a Value stocks) tend to outperform low (Growth stocks) - the factor is high book-to=market minus low, or HML; and that small cap. stocks tend to outperform large caps.(SMB). The 5-Factor version of their model adds factors reflecting profitability (robust operating profitabilty minus weak, or RMW) and an investment factor (conservative minus aggressive US investment style, CMA).

The model fits a linear relationship between RmMFr and these paramenters. Professor French maintains a database of the factor values by month since 1963. Our analysis of the 3 and 5 factor models adds one additional factor, the constant alpha representing the overall gain, and uses the data to find what weighting should be applied to each factor to best match the return measured.

The literature on the Fama-French factor model is extensive, including articles in investopedia and wikipedia.

Seeker's output shows the overall coefficient of correlation, R2, which indicates the proportion of return that may be attributed to the factors (the difference reflecting management skill). It then shows, for each factor, the weighting coefficient applied to each factor to get the best match; the standard error for that weighting, which indicates how narrow the match is; and the probability that the weighting for the parameter is significant.


Why Correlation?

Correlation can be causal, or coincidental, like hemlines and the outcome of SuperBowl. When used between entities in similar environments, there is more likely to be a causal connection. The analysis of significance shown on this page is one measure of how strong a causal relationship might be. 

Correlation challenges sector analysis as a measure of group performance. Sector definitions are often historical and outgrown by the development of conglomerate corporations. It is hard to find a one-phrase description for these businesses that is not so wide as to be useless. On the other hand correlation measures actual performance, and we applied Seeker to our group of Healthcare stocks to see the difference. You can see the results in the section "Using Seeker". Many of the stocks in the sector correlated very well, but there are many that do not; in some cases performing in the exact opposite of each other!

For that reason, our Portfolio Style Analysis uses changes in correlation with industry and sector groups as a better definition of how their relationship is changing over time. 

  • Seekers' ability to correlate entire industries enables you to mine 4M combinations of stocks and indexes.
  • Correlations that you find present opportunities for hedging or beta management.
  • Phase changes and auto-correlation let you look for cyclicality and leading indicators.
  • Seeker's approach to portfolio analysis bases style-trend analysis on performance in relation to indexes, not stock composition. This avoids the distortion of multi-industry conglomerates.
