HOW CAN SEEKER HELP ME?

CORRELATION

Correlation allows you to measure two investment entities, a pair of tocks, or a stock and an index, or a stock and an ETF, or two portfolios over time. A correlation coefficient of 1 indicates that they are perfectly matched: 0 indicates that they have no relationship; -1 indicates that they move in opposite directions to each other.

In returns-based style analysis, correlation can be useful by allowing comparison between portfolios and indexes, for example. Indexes are defined in terms of their market composition, and if a portfolio can be correlated with an index, then the portfolio may be characterized by it and have an exposure in similar markets. This is where returns-based analysis differs from holdings-based analysis; it is defined by behavior rather than the consituent stocks.

Many investors like to use sectors to identify investments that they believe will move together in market changes; the difficulty is that today's conglomerates are in so many markets! Correlation matches stocks not on a historical definition of their business, but on actual performance. Our comparison of the healthcare market in 'Using Seeker' illustrates how diverse sector performance is!

Seeker correlates combinations of entities over an time period within its data range, but also breaks the period into subintervals so that you can see how correlation changed over the period, with a statistical summary of the changes. 

if you want to find a stock that matches your portfolio, or to see if it really only matches an index, or you are looking for a match in a past market period, correlation is very effective, but it has other properties. If you advance the timing of one of the two entities you are researching,, and you find that they match better when one of them is advanced, you might have an index or stock that is a leading indicator. Seeker lets you investigate offsets of up to 2 years, and you can run a comparison of your favorite stock against all 1600 indexes in seconds to find out.

A final feature of correlation is autocorrelation in which you correlate a stock or index with itself, and change the timing. If you find that the correlation improves if one is advanced then you have an entity that is cyclical, and so its performance can be predicted. 

Seeker processes approximately 40,000 correlations/second, so it responds while you are still thinking. Very productive!

FACTOR ANALYSIS

Correlation measures similarity of performance but can not explain why it happens: we can find which stocks tend to have similar performance, or which indexes behave like selected stocks, and we can show how these similarlties are preserved over time, but not why.

Regression enables us to compare performance against parameters that might be contributory, and is used to try to isolate whether changes in performance are unique to the entity and its management, or may be explained by factors that are known to influence performance, such as the fact that small cap stocks tend to outperform large caps. This analysis depends on defining which factors might be useful, and on data that enables us to isolate their effects.

There are a number of factor models; Seeker uses the Fama-French model developed by Professors Eugene Fama and Kenneth French at the University of Chicago. This 3-factor model originated from the observation that all stocks tend to benefit from a rising tide; that small cap stocks grow faster than large caps; and that value stocks tend to outperform growth stocks over time. 

The resulting research at the University of Chicago produced a linear definition of performance in which the contribution of the factors could be isolated, and a library of historical data that measures the performance over time of stocks that reflect each factor. The model and the data enable the performance of a portfolio to be analyzed over time to measure how much of a contribution each factor may have provided.

The original 3-factor model was enhanced to 5 in 2015, and Seeker supports both.

The analysis provides

  • R2, a coefficient that indicates the overall contribution of the factors to the result.
  • the intercept of the regression line that represents alpha, the annualized excess gain over the risk free investment rate.
  • an overall measurement of the extent to which the performance of the portfolio can be attributed to the factors, The remainder is a measurement of the manager's skill.
  • the weighting to be applied to each factor,
  • the "standard error" for each weighting to measure how consistent the analysis is,
  • the probability that the weighting is significant (typically around 95%).

So what are the factors? Their names are cryptic - in the 3-factor model they are:

  • RmMRfr    the Return (monthly) of the portfolio Minus the "risk free rate" (of short-term Treasuries). Measures sensitivity to the market as a whole.
  • SMB    Small cap Minus Big cap. Measures sensitivity to small caps.
  •  HML   High Minus Low. Measures high book to price (Value) minus low (Growth)

In 5 factor analysis, the additional factors are"

  • RMW    Robust profitability Minus Weak. 
  • CMA    Conservative Minus Aggressive approachour equities to investment in the US.

The result of the analysis shows R2, a measurement of the extent tothe  which performance can be attributed to the factors (the remainder being a reflection of management skill). Then for each factor, plus the gain, it shows the weighting for each factor that was optimal; the standard error (similar to a standard deviation of the weighting); and the statistical significance of the result for the factor, measured as the percentage probability that the contribution of the factor is not random.

Because Seeker supports analysis on large groups, you can run factor analysis on the entire Wilshire 5000, in seconds, and sort the results by the factors, to see the populations that match each. 

WHAT MIGHT I LOOK FOR?

STOCKS

Is correlation a consistent indicator?
How does it vary over time?
Are there other stocks that correlate with my portfolio?
Are any of them leading indicators of performance for my portfolio? 
Is there any index that is a leading indicator? 
Which indexes behave like stocks that I like? 
How does their correlation match over time? 
Are any of the stocks or indexes cyclical? Over what period? 
How consistent are my watch group stocks? 

INDEXES

Which indexes correlate with stocks that I like?
Are indexes less volatile correlators than other stocks?
How well do individual indexes within a sector or value group match the average? 
When do they diverge (and why)? 
Which track closest to the average of the group? 
What can I learn from stock and index correlation in volatile markets?

PORTFOLIOS

Does factor analysis reflect skill or coincidence in my portfolio?
Which indexes suggest higher levels of management performance?
Can I add stocks to my portfolio based on factor analysis?
How can I find additional stocks that correlate, or don't correlate with my portfolio?
How closely does my portfolio or stock watchlist track stock and index sector groups? 
Are there index groups that define my portfolio's style?
If so, how does it vary over time?
